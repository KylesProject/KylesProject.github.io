Part One

Why Residential Schools Were Created

A residential school facility. Imposing institutional buildings were deliberately located far from Indigenous communities.

"The goal was to teach Indigenous children European values, Christianity, and English or French, erasing everything they already were."

01

Racist Beliefs and Colonial Ideas

Many European settlers believed their culture, religion, and way of life were superior to those of Indigenous peoples. These racist beliefs led to the idea that Indigenous peoples were uncivilized and needed to be changed to fit European norms. Politicians, missionaries, and settlers often described Indigenous peoples as obstacles to national progress, and Indigenous knowledge and traditions were excluded from school curricula entirely.

Racist beliefs that Indigenous cultures were primitive led many Canadian leaders in the 1800s to conclude that Indigenous societies needed to be replaced by European culture. Because of this mindset, the government saw assimilation as necessary for building a modern Canadian nation. Residential schools were designed to accelerate this process by separating children from their families and reshaping their identities.

02

Assimilation as Government Policy

The Canadian government adopted a policy of assimilation, meaning Indigenous peoples were expected to abandon their languages, traditions, and identities and adopt European culture instead. Residential schools were created as the key tool to force this assimilation, particularly targeting children who were seen as easier to influence than adults.

Many officials believed that Indigenous parents were incapable of properly raising their children according to European standards. This assumption justified the forced removal of children from their homes. Authorities claimed that Indigenous communities promoted lifestyles incompatible with Western education, Christianity, and agriculture, and believed the only solution was to educate children in controlled environments away from their families.

03

Laws That Enabled the System

Racist attitudes also influenced the legal framework that allowed the residential school system to exist. Policies under the Indian Act gave the federal government extensive power over Indigenous communities, including authority over education. These laws treated Indigenous peoples as wards of the state rather than equal citizens, and Indigenous peoples faced limitations on movement, voting, and participation in Canadian society.

Because of this legal structure, Indigenous parents had little control over their children's schooling. Parents who resisted sending their children could face punishment or legal consequences.

Residential schools were operated through a partnership between the Canadian government and Christian churches, including the Catholic, Anglican, Methodist, and Presbyterian churches. The government provided funding while churches managed the schools. Church leaders believed Indigenous spiritual traditions were pagan or sinful and needed to be eliminated. Christian teachings were used to convince students that abandoning their traditional beliefs was necessary for salvation, treating Indigenous spiritual systems as inferior rather than recognizing them as legitimate religions.

Part Two

Life Inside Residential Schools

What children experienced inside these institutions was devastating across every aspect of their lives.

Children in a classroom under the watch of a religious instructor. Academic learning was minimal while religious instruction was constant.
A packed assembly hall. Overcrowding was common in underfunded schools, contributing to the spread of disease.
🏠

Separation from Families

Indigenous children were forcibly taken from their homes and placed in residential schools, often far from their communities. Communication was extremely limited for most students. Letters were sometimes censored or restricted, and visits home were rare. Some children spent years without seeing their parents or siblings, which deepened the cultural and emotional divide between children and their communities.

Indigenous children were frequently separated from their siblings within the schools as well. Boys and girls lived in separate areas with limited contact, which weakened family bonds, made children more dependent on school authorities, and prevented them from supporting each other emotionally.

When students eventually returned home, many felt disconnected from their culture and family life. They struggled to readjust because they had been taught to reject Indigenous traditions. Some no longer spoke their original language fluently, making communication with elders difficult and creating tension and misunderstanding within families.

✂️

Loss of Culture and Identity

Children were punished for speaking their Indigenous languages or practicing cultural traditions. Traditional ceremonies, beliefs, and cultural practices were banned or discouraged entirely.

Children arriving at residential schools were subjected to processes designed to erase their previous identities. Staff cut their hair, replaced traditional clothing with European-style uniforms, and assigned them numbers or Christian names. These changes removed visible signs of Indigenous identity and reinforced the belief that Indigenous culture should be erased.

⚠️

Poor Conditions and Abuse

Many schools were overcrowded and underfunded. Food was inadequate, causing malnutrition and health problems. Buildings were poorly maintained, and children often lacked proper clothing and bedding. Diseases such as tuberculosis spread easily in these unsanitary conditions. Medical care was limited or neglected entirely, and many children died while attending residential schools and were buried in unmarked graves.

Racist ideas about control and authority were used to justify the strict discipline at these schools. Many administrators believed Indigenous children needed harsher discipline because they were supposedly less civilized. Punishments included corporal punishment, confinement, or deprivation of privileges. These practices created an environment of fear and obedience. Many students also experienced emotional abuse including humiliation, isolation, and constant criticism of their Indigenous identity, and numerous survivors reported sexual abuse by staff members.

Government funding for the schools was extremely limited, and administrators sometimes prioritized cost savings over student welfare. In some documented cases, students were used in nutrition experiments without proper consent.

🌾

Limited Education and Forced Labour

Academic education was minimal compared to regular Canadian schools. Many policymakers believed Indigenous peoples should be trained to fit into the lowest levels of the Canadian economy. Boys were commonly taught farming or manual trades while girls were trained in cooking, cleaning, and sewing. This reflected the racist assumption that Indigenous students were not capable of higher education or professional careers.

Many residential schools relied on student labour to keep the institutions operating. Children were required to perform chores including farming, laundry, cooking, cleaning, and building maintenance. These tasks were presented as part of their training but often replaced actual classroom learning, allowing schools to reduce operating costs while exploiting the children in their care.

Boys performing agricultural labour on school grounds. Forced work was framed as vocational training but served primarily to reduce operating costs.
Part Three

Long-Term Effects on Individuals and Communities

The trauma caused by the residential school system did not end when the schools closed. Its effects have reverberated across generations.

"Trauma experienced by survivors affected families and communities for generations, shaping parenting, relationships, and community well-being."

Psychological and Emotional Impact

Being separated from family at a young age created lasting feelings of abandonment and loneliness. Without the comfort of their communities or cultural traditions, many students struggled with identity confusion. Students were often taught that their culture and identity were inferior, causing feelings of shame and a deep loss of self-worth. Many survivors struggled with self-esteem and cultural disconnection well into adulthood, and these emotional wounds often lasted long after they left the schools.

Intergenerational Trauma

Survivors often carried emotional and psychological trauma into adulthood. This trauma affected parenting, family relationships, and community stability, and the impacts were passed down to later generations, affecting Indigenous communities for decades. This is known as intergenerational trauma.

Loss of Language and Cultural Knowledge

Because children were separated from elders, communities lost language speakers, knowledge keepers, and cultural teachers. Cultural traditions that depended on intergenerational learning were severely disrupted. This weakened cultural continuity across many Indigenous nations and the damage persists to this day.

Social and Economic Challenges

Limited education and trauma made it harder for many survivors to find stable employment or pursue higher education. These barriers contributed to cycles of poverty and inequality that continue to affect many communities today. Communities faced ongoing hardship because many survivors received only minimal schooling and experienced lasting psychological harm.

Racism in Broader Society

Discrimination against Indigenous peoples outside the schools reinforced the harmful messages students received inside them. Indigenous communities often faced segregation, limited employment opportunities, and social prejudice in Canadian society. Even after leaving residential schools, many former students encountered racism that prevented them from fully participating in Canadian life.

Government officials sometimes portrayed residential schools as charitable institutions helping Indigenous children. This narrative was used to justify the system to the broader Canadian public, and many Canadians were unaware of the abuse and poor conditions inside the schools, limiting public criticism for many decades.

Indigenous leaders and parents resisted the residential school system from the beginning. Some families attempted to hide their children or refused to send them to school, while others protested the harsh treatment and poor conditions reported by students. Despite this resistance, government authorities continued enforcing attendance laws.

Part Four

Recognition and Reconciliation

Today, the residential school system is widely recognized as a major injustice in Canadian history. In recent decades, survivor testimonies have played an important role in educating Canadians about the realities of the system. Survivors have shared their experiences through public inquiries, documentaries, books, and community events, helping expose the harmful impact of racist policies that shaped the schools.

An Orange Shirt Day display. Observed every September 30th, Orange Shirt Day honours survivors and commemorates the children who never came home.

Survivors' Voices

Survivors have bravely shared their stories through public inquiries, documentaries, books, and community events. Their testimonies have been central to raising awareness and holding institutions accountable for what happened.

Education and Commemoration

Modern education in Canada increasingly includes lessons about residential schools and Indigenous history. Schools, universities, and public institutions now work to ensure students understand the historical context and consequences of these policies. This education is intended to prevent similar injustices in the future.

Cultural Revitalization

Communities across Canada are working to revitalize languages, restore cultural traditions, and reconnect younger generations with their heritage. These efforts directly challenge the racist beliefs that originally attempted to erase Indigenous identity.

Resistance and Resilience

Despite the system's cruelty, many Indigenous families resisted from the very beginning. Some parents refused to send their children to school, while others protested poor treatment and harsh conditions. Indigenous resilience has always been part of this history.

Acknowledging Racism

Recognizing the role that racist beliefs and colonial attitudes played in creating the residential school system is an essential step toward meaningful reconciliation. National discussions about reconciliation aim to address the full scope of historical harm caused by the system.

Building New Relationships

The path forward involves building respectful relationships between Indigenous and non-Indigenous peoples, grounded in truth, accountability, and a shared commitment to justice.
